Historical Source Analysis

This is an image of a Mooar plant in Keokuk, Iowa belonging to a company called DuPont. The factory began operation in 1890. The image of the factory above can be classified as a primary source, as it is an image of the original factory in 1890. This factory began operation during the Second Industrial Revolution, which occurred between 1870 and 1914. The Second Industrial Revolution saw the advancement of multiple industrial techniques, technologies, and infrastructure, such as the mass use of machinery in factories, the expansion of railroad tracks, and more.

Steel Production

Mass production of steel began during the Second Industrial Revolution, enabled by the invention of the Bessemer process by Sir Henry Bessemer several decades prior (Wikipedia, 2025). This meant cheaper construction of various industrial and commercial buildings, infrastructure, and machinery. There were also several advancements made in chemical processes, resulting in further mass production of various materials and products (Wikipedia, 2025). This tells us that the plant in the source was constructed during a period of rapid industrial expansion, when factories and mechanized production became increasingly widespread. Because of this industrial expansion, the use of fossil fuels and chemical processes that produce air pollution, such as the Bessemer Process, became increasingly common.

Railroad Building

Due to railroad expansion in that period, the use of coal-powered trains was greatly increased, alongside the use of coal-powered factories and powerplants. This eventually resulted in large amounts of CO2 being released into the atmosphere. In 1850, a total of 204.63Mt of CO2 emissions were produced. In comparison, a total of approximately 3.40 Gt of CO2 was produced in 1914, the year the Second Industrial Revolution ended. In 2022 this number had grown to 37.10 Gt of CO2 (Climate Watch, n.d). All of this evidence points to the Second Industrial Revolution being a major turning point when it comes to carbon emissions, with emissions being increased approximately 17-fold.

Mooar Plant

All of this information tells us that the Mooar plant shown in the source image is a direct representation of the industrialisation in the Second Industrial Revolution. As a factory built during the Revolution, it likely had a heavy reliance on fossil fuels and air pollution producing procedures and processes. This factory was just one of many that played a role in shaping the way we produce and transport goods, eventually leading to the climate issues we all face today.
